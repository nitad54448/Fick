Onsager Multi-Component Diffusion Solver • v8.5

1. Overview

This tool simulates multi-component cation diffusion in oxide crystals using the Onsager transport formalism. Unlike simple Fickian diffusion where species diffuse independently, real crystals exhibit correlated motion. Every jump requires a vacancy, and vacancies are shared by all species, producing cross-coupling between fluxes.

The solver calculates concentration profiles, fluxes, and time evolution using concentration‑dependent diffusivities, full Onsager coupling, and optional thermodynamic interactions through a Regular Solution model.

2. Physical Models

2.1 Onsager Flux Equation

For each species i, the flux is governed by gradients of all concentrations:

$$ J_i = -\sum_j D_{ij} \nabla C_j $$
  • Diagonal terms: diffusion of species i down its own gradient.
  • Off‑diagonal terms: cross‑coupling due to vacancy sharing.

These effects cause phenomena like uphill diffusion (flow against a gradient) and vacancy wind (dragging slower ions by fast-moving ones).

2.2 Why the Vacancy Wind Exists

In vacancy-mediated materials, a jump of species A to the right implies the vacancy moves left. If one species jumps much faster than others, its motion creates a net vacancy flow—a vacancy wind. Slower species can then be swept in the opposite direction.

2.3 Manning–Darken Connection

In binary systems, Onsager theory reduces to the classic Darken and Manning expressions for interdiffusion. Manning’s correlation factor accounts for vacancy drag effects. In systems with 3+ species, interdiffusion ceases to be a single number—hence the full matrix formulation used here.

3. The Simulation Matrices

Transport is controlled by two key matrices: the kinetic Q-matrix and the thermodynamic Ω‑matrix.

3.1 Kinetics: The Q‑Matrix

The activation energy for a jump depends on who is jumping (the diffuser) and who surrounds the site (the environment). Thus, for each species pair (i,j) we define:

$$ Q_{ij} = \text{barrier for species } i \text{ moving in environment dominated by } j $$

Why the Q‑Matrix Can Be Asymmetric

In real materials, diffusion barriers depend on ionic size, charge, electronic configuration, and local strain. These effects are not symmetric:

  • Mg moving in a Ni‑rich environment is not equivalent to Ni moving in a Mg‑rich one.
  • Charge‑state or spin‑state differences alter saddle‑point energies differently for each ion.
  • Local distortions (e.g., Zn tetrahedral tendency; Cu Jahn–Teller distortion) produce direction‑dependent effects.

Interpretation of Qij

Row = Diffuser, Column = Environment. A larger value means a higher activation barrier and slower jumps.

The effective tracer diffusivity used in the solver is:

$$ D_i^*(x) = D_{0,i}\, \exp\left(-\frac{\sum_j C_j(x)\, Q_{ij}}{k_B T}\right) $$

3.2 Thermodynamics: The Ω‑Matrix

The Ω‑matrix defines enthalpic mixing interactions using a regular‑solution model:

$$ H_{mix} = \sum_{i \neq j} \Omega_{ij} X_i X_j $$

Unlike Q, these interactions are fully symmetric: Ωij = Ωji.

Positive Ω → demixing; negative Ω → ordering. Large Ω can produce strong chemical driving forces and significant departures from ideal Fickian behavior.
